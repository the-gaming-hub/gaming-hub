If you're new to gaming, you may be perplexed by all the terminology and wondering whether you're playing at an appropriate FPS.

Importance Of FPS In Gaming


A decade ago, memes circulated about the limitations of human vision in terms of frames per second (FPS) in video games. Console players, who were then limited to 30 frames per second, frequently asserted that this frame rate was the absolute maximum that the human eye could perceive. However, the truth is more convoluted. While our eyes can only detect approximately 10-12 distinct frames per second, higher frame rates are viewed as smoother motion.

Nonetheless, there is a clear difference in frame rates, including 24 FPS, 30 FPS, and the more fluid 60 FPS. For example, when comparing 24 FPS with 60 FPS, the difference in smoothness is noticeable.

In the demonstration below, you will see how things appear at various frame rates. The jerky motion at 24 FPS may create the appearance of slower movement than the smoother transitions at 60 FPS, despite the objects reaching the screen edges at the same time.



Given these data, you might conclude that 60 FPS is the ideal frame rate for gaming. While gaming at 60 frames per second clearly provides a smoother experience, other elements must be considered before drawing any conclusions.

Ideal FPS for Gaming



For many years, gaming at 30 frames per second was plenty for everyone. However, as technology evolved, gamers and developers attempted to take use of it. 60 FPS quickly became the new standard for single-player games, while 120 or higher became the norm for multiplayer games, notably first-person shooters.

This may lead you to believe that more is always better, and while you will undoubtedly have a smoother experience, it does not necessarily imply that it is the ideal option for you.

Let's go over this in greater detail.

Is 60 FPS Enough For Today's Games?



The previous threshold of 30 FPS is still sufficient to keep a game watchable. Anything below that can appear unsteady, and most gamers will struggle to enjoy their games at a lower frame rate. The modern cinematography and animation standard is 24 frames per second. However, there are experiences that do not require human engagement. As a result, our eyes are more relaxed, allowing us to appreciate the film at that frame rate.

The new norm of 60 FPS took a little longer to reach for console gamers, as only the most recent console generations, the PlayStation 5 and Xbox Series X, support native 60 FPS. PC players, on the other hand, have been playing at 60 frames per second for nearly a decade.

For the majority of players, 60 FPS is the optimal frame rate. This is due to not only the smoothness of the visuals displayed, but also the fact that 60Hz displays are the most widely available. Furthermore, purchasing a GPU capable of producing 60 frames per second in a video game is now relatively simple and inexpensive.

Is 120+ FPS The Best For Modern Games?



At 120 FPS, things appear different. Of course, the image is smoother than at 60 FPS, but most gamers still find playing at 120 FPS to be excessive. Many players struggle to discern the difference between 120 and 60 FPS, making it difficult to create a compelling case for playing at 120 FPS.

Nonetheless, given that higher framerates significantly reduce input lag, particularly in multiplayer games, more is clearly better. As previously stated, gaming at 120 FPS necessitates more expensive hardware, particularly if you intend to run a graphical powerhouse like Control at that frame rate.

First, you'll need a really strong GPU, although depending on the game, you might be able to get away with something a little less expensive. You will then need to choose a monitor that not only supports 120 Hz but also a vertical synchronization technique that is compatible with your GPU. This is only applicable if you wish to reap the full benefits of 120 FPS gaming. If you want to game with NVIDIA's GPUs, you may notice a minor cost rise due to the G-Sync technology, which requires a special license from the monitor maker. AMD's FreeSync technology makes things slightly cheaper.

120 To 360+ FPS


If you're willing to spend as much money as necessary to have the 120 FPS experience, go for it, but we still have to tell you the main disadvantage of 120 FPS gaming. The truth is that with such a price increase, the eye test cannot produce the desired results. You may only notice it when playing an online game that requires a high frame rate.

We are primarily talking to shooter games like Counter-Strike: Global Offensive. In those instances, you will have a clear advantage over an opponent who plays at a lower frame rate because you can see and respond to them faster. We're talking milliseconds here, which most casual players are unlikely to benefit from. Competitive gamers, on the other hand, might enjoy a better experience at 120 FPS, while the top eSports competitors go much higher, to 240 FPS or 360 FPS. These frame rates are not currently popular, but will be in the future.
