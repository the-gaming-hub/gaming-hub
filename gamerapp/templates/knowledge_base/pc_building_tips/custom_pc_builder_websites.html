FAQs


Should I verify the compatibility of PC parts before assembling my PC?

Ensuring compatibility is crucial, especially regarding the motherboard. Before proceeding with any purchases or assembly, it's essential to check compatibility to avoid potential issues such as the system failing to boot.

How does the process work if I select my own components for the PC build on these websites?

If you choose your desired PC build and customize the parts to fit your preferences, the websites will handle the assembly for you.

Is it essential to invest in an extended warranty?

Typically, standard warranties cover builds and parts for one to three years. Opting out of an extended warranty can help you avoid additional expenses.

What is the typical lifespan of a gaming PC once it's built?

The longevity of a custom gaming PC varies based on its hardware specifications. Generally, lower-end gaming PCs may last around 3 years, while higher-end builds can maintain satisfactory performance for over 8 years. Additionally, the lifespan of individual components varies; for instance, GPUs may require upgrades every 5 to 7 years, whereas well-maintained motherboards can endure for up to 20 years.

What is the typical lifespan of a CPU used for gaming?

When a CPU is not subjected to overclocking and is adequately cooled, it can maintain functionality for gaming purposes for approximately 5 to 10 years or more.

Is it advisable to leave a PC running continuously for 24 hours a day?

Keeping your PC operational 24/7 can potentially lead to wear and tear, particularly due to the frequent cycling of power. However, if you have ongoing tasks or processes that require the computer's continuous operation, it's generally acceptable to leave it running. It's essential to maintain good ventilation in the room where the PC is located and regularly monitor the temperature of its components to prevent overheating issues.
