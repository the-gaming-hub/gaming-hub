Not Reading The Manuals


This is a huge one: read the manuals. Every part's manual is crucial, but the most important ones would definitely be your motherboard manual, your case manual, your PSU manual and your CPU manual.

Unless you're a PC building specialist, you should read all of your manuals to ensure that you don't overlook any important information while building your PC. Mistakes during the construction process can necessitate disassembling and starting over, or, in the worst-case scenario, hardware damage. If you want to learn more about PC building, see our How To Build A PC guide.

Selecting Incompatible Parts


If you're using one of our builds, don't worry: we've already checked. Otherwise, One of the most common mistakes New PC builders make is selecting incompatible components. For example, they may use the incorrect CPU or RAM with their motherboard.

For example, an Intel Core i7-9700K CPU and an LGA 1150 motherboard will not function since they are incompatible. If you want to use an Intel Core i7-9700K processor, you should go with an LGA 1151 socket motherboard.

Furthermore, matching DDR3 RAM with a motherboard that only supports DDR4 is a rookie mistake that can be avoided by using a service like PCPartPicker to select your components or consulting a professional PC builder before placing your order.

Moreover, using a service like PCPartPicker is useful for identifying compatible parts, but there is another factor to consider when matching PC hardware: bottlenecking. Bottlenecking is defined as a component that limits processing power or graphical performance, and it often occurs when two components' maximum capabilities are not equal. Fortunately, there are various online PC bottleneck calculators available to guarantee you're purchasing appropriate hardware.

Forgetting Motherboard Standoff Screws


When opening your PC case for the first time, take note of the holes designed for securing your motherboard. However, avoid directly screwing the motherboard into these holes on the metallic interior, as it can lead to shorts, potentially damaging your hardware. Instead, use standoffs provided in the bag of screws that comes with your motherboard.


These standoffs elevate the motherboard, preventing direct contact with the metal case and reducing the risk of shorts. Ensure to install the standoffs before placing the motherboard in the case. Adding standoffs and risers is a crucial safety measure, creating a slight elevation that shields the motherboard's solder points and circuit traces from contact with the metal case. Always prioritize safety during installation!

Building On An Unsafe Surface


When embarking on the process of building your PC, selecting an appropriate workspace is crucial for a smooth assembly experience. Begin by identifying a spacious, well-lit, and secure area - ideally, a large table or desk. While it's technically possible to build a computer in a smaller space, opting for a larger surface proves advantageous due to the multitude of cables, screws, and components that require organization and accessibility during assembly.

It is imperative to avoid surfaces with the potential for static electricity, such as carpets and rugs. The presence of static electricity poses a risk to sensitive components. If you have access to a table, you're in the clear; simply ensure all materials are placed directly on the table surface. However, if a large table is unavailable or you must work on a static conductive surface, consider utilizing an anti-static wristband as a precautionary measure. The Rosewill Antistatic Wrist Band is a budget-friendly option that effectively minimizes the risk of unintentional component damage by preventing the build-up of static charge.


In summary, prioritize a spacious, organized workspace on a non-conductive surface, such as wood, and exercise caution regarding static electricity. An anti-static wrist strap is a valuable investment for grounding yourself, enhancing the safety of your components during the PC building process.

Forgetting The Motherboard IO Shield


Ensuring a smooth PC building process requires attention to detail, and one crucial step is the timely installation of the motherboard's I/O shield. This small yet essential component is provided with your motherboard and should be inserted into your case before mounting the motherboard. Failure to do so can lead to complications, making the process more challenging, if not impossible, once the motherboard is in place.

But what is the I/O Shield? if you dont know The I/O shield is a part what allows for optimum air flow and grounding for the motherboard ports. The I/O shield helps ensure the motherboard is installed correctly and properly aligned with the case.


If you overlook installing the I/O shield initially, rectifying the mistake necessitates unscrewing and disassembling components or maneuvering around the already mounted motherboard and GPU. To avoid this inconvenience, remember to install the I/O shield before securing the motherboard to the case. This proactive approach simplifies the assembly process and prevents the need for extensive rework or adjustments later on.

Installing Memory Improperly


When working with an even number of memory modules, such as 2 or 4, it is crucial to ensure proper spacing between them. Refer to your motherboard's manual to identify the correct slots for your RAM modules. Proper dual-channel configuration necessitates spacing between RAM sticks. Additionally, when installing RAM, make sure to push it all the way down until you hear a click, indicating a secure attachment.


It's important to note that, with the introduction of DDR5, mixing different capacities in channels or banks is currently not supported. If you have a kit of, for example, 2x8GB, you cannot add another set of 2x16GB later. When planning a DDR5 build, choose the appropriate capacity for your needs. While some DDR5 motherboards may receive BIOS updates in the future to provide more flexibility in memory capacity combinations, it's advisable to check with the manufacturer for the latest information on compatibility and updates.

Fans Pushing Air In Wrong Direction


Ensuring proper airflow within your PC case is vital for effective cooling, especially when installing additional fans. While the fans that come with your case generally push air in the correct direction, it's essential to pay attention when adding your own. To prevent overheating, all fans, whether pre-installed or added, should be configured to push air toward the back or top of your case. Incorrectly installing case fans can lead to system overheating, a frustrating experience for any PC builder.


When dealing with pre-installed fans, they typically draw air in from the front and expel it from the rear, promoting effective airflow. However, if you decide to incorporate additional case fans into your build, take the time to understand their directional airflow. Coordinating the movement of cool air from one direction to the expulsion of warm air in the opposite direction enhances overall cooling efficiency. Therefore, before adding more fans to your PC, ensure you are familiar with the airflow direction of each fan to optimize your system's cooling capabilities.

Incorrect CPU Installation


For novice PC builders unfamiliar with the process, the potential mistake of incorrectly installing the CPU can lead to damaging or breaking its pins, depending on the force applied. Fortunately, determining the correct orientation for CPU installation is straightforward. Processors are equipped with markings on their corners, guiding users on the proper alignment with the motherboard's CPU socket. Aligning the marked corners ensures a secure fit without the risk of damaging the pins.

Installing CPU, RAM, And Cooler After Installing Motherboard


Did you know that, in most cases, you may install your CPU, RAM, and even your (Air) cooler on your motherboard before putting it in your case?

You are not need to do these steps initially, but doing so will make the PC building process much easier in the long run, so I strongly advise you to do so.

Forgetting To Remove Plastic Cover Or Apply Thermal Paste On New CPU Cooler


Thermal paste, often known as TIM (thermal interface material), is an essential aspect of any PC setup.

Essentially, it allows your CPU to more efficiently transport heat to your cooler, where it can be dispersed to keep temperatures low and performance consistent.


Most CPU coolers, including AMD and Intel stock coolers, come with pre-applied TIM. However, it may be covered with plastic, which you need remove before installing the cooler.

Furthermore, for the rare CPU coolers that do not have their own TIM, you must provide and apply your own thermal paste.

Plugging Cables Into The Wrong Ports


This error can be easily avoided if you verify all of the cords before proceeding. All cords must be connected to the correct input or your system will not switch on, and you risk damage the components. Plug-in locations are always listed in the manual.

A typical error among first-time builders, but make sure you connect your HDMI/DVI/etc connections to your graphics card rather than your motherboard's inbuilt video ports.

Adding Too Much Thermal Paste


Careful consideration during thermal paste installation is crucial to prevent potential mess and ensure optimal thermal performance for your PC. Applying thermal paste requires a level of precision and finesse, requiring a delicate balance. Excessive paste can lead to seepage, causing damage, especially when dealing with liquid metal compounds. Conversely, too little thermal paste may result in overheating issues, which may go unnoticed.


For an ideal application, experts recommend placing a rice grain-sized drop of thermal paste in the center of the CPU's back. When installing the heatsink, the paste should evenly spread out. Overdoing thermal paste can compromise thermal performance, so it's advised to apply it in small amounts approximately the size of a pea or slightly smaller. Ensure even distribution by placing the cooler down evenly. Some coolers come with pre-applied thermal paste, and if this is the case, refrain from adding more; simply mount the cooler as usual, and the paste will apply automatically. By adhering to these guidelines, you can avoid potential complications and achieve efficient thermal conductivity for your PC components.

Buying Cheap PSUs


There's a difference between buying an affordable power supply and buying a cheap and low-quality one. However, if you're on a tight budget and the rest of your PC components aren't particularly "power-hungry," it's fine to spend a little less on your power supply.

One of the best ways to pick a power supply is to roughly calculate how much power your components will consume. For this, you can use a PSU calculator tool like the one that OuterVision offers, but there are several other online power supply calculator options. Also, if you're using PCPartPicker to build your PC, they give you a rough estimate of how much power your computer will consume and which power supply can accommodate all the components.


Another important thing is to check your power supply's +12v railing rating amperage. This is where your graphics card will draw its power, which is the most power-hungry component of your PC. Again, OuterVision's CPU calculator will tell you this amperage rating.

Furthermore, before buying a new power supply, check if it's a high-quality PSU or not at HardwareSecrets. They test the most popular power supplies on the market and give you a review based on their thorough testing.

Not Updating The BIOS Before Booting(on certain Motherboards)


This is only an issue with motherboards and sockets that support various generations of CPUs.

Many older AM4 motherboards receive BIOS upgrades that enable compatibility with more recent models of AM4 socket Ryzen CPUs; however, these updates are not always implemented before you, the end user, receive your motherboard.

Nothing bad will happen if you boot a computer with a BIOS-incompatible but physically compatible CPU in the socket.

However, you will be unable to accomplish anything because the motherboard does not recognize the CPU you have installed.

You can either contact your motherboard manufacturer to request a loaner CPU to do the BIOS update, or you can update the BIOS without using a CPU if your motherboard supports it.

Not Keeping Hold Of Your Warranty


All warranty information, manuals, etc.? Save them and keep them in an easily accessible location. If your products can be registered online through the manufacturer's website, do so as well. This ensures that if anything happens to your components, you can obtain RMAs and repairs at a lower cost and with more ease.
