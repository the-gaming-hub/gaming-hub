PC Overview


Building a gaming PC for less than $500 is pretty tough because you have to make every dollar count to get decent performance. If you're new to building custom PCs, it might seem a bit overwhelming at first, but don't worry! We've put together an easy guide to help you build the best gaming PC for under $500. It'll make sure you have all the parts you need to create a great entry-level gaming setup that suits your needs. Plus, with this affordable build, you'll still be able to play a wide range of games.

In today's world of technology, you might be surprised at what you can get with a $500 budget. Gone are the days when you could only afford consoles or APUs. Now, you can build a PC under $500 that comes with its own dedicated graphics card. While many of the components in this PC are considered entry-level and from older generations, we still think they do a good job for the price. Just remember to keep your expectations realistic at this price point.

If you want more recommendations for budget gaming PCs at different price ranges, be sure to check out our page dedicated to budget gaming PCs.

PC hardware deals


When it comes to building your next gaming PC, scoring deals on components is key to getting more bang for your buck. Whether it's GPUs, CPUs, coolers, motherboards, power supplies, or cases, there are always deals to be found. You don't have to wait for special sales either; discounts are available year-round, allowing you to save some money here and there. So keep your eyes peeled for those bargains!

PC Budget


Putting together the ultimate custom PC for under $500 was definitely a challenge. We had to make a lot of trade-offs to squeeze out the best performance possible from this build. But we made it happen! With this budget, it's totally doable to create a gaming PC with its own dedicated graphics card. And let me tell you, the difference in performance between a $400 build and a $500 build is huge once you step away from integrated graphics. At the $500 mark, we were even able to include spacious M.2 SSDs and 16GB of RAM.

When it came to choosing a case, we had to be mindful of our budget. While we opted for affordable options, we still made sure to select a case that's easy to work with for first-time builders. Plus, we wanted to ensure good airflow to keep everything running smoothly.

Performance


With this $500 build, you can expect smooth gaming at 1080p with 60 frames per second on many AAA titles, even at high settings. Plus, it'll breeze through popular esports games effortlessly, giving you plenty of entry-level options to explore. If you're into esports, you can even achieve high frame rates by tweaking the settings a bit. Overall, this budget PC is quite versatile. We've boosted its performance with 16GB of RAM, which is perfect for gaming, and included an SSD for fast boot times and snappy performance in both Windows and your games. Plus, the ample RAM provides some future-proofing for your system.

AMD vs Intel


The $500 gaming PC build makes smart use of budget-friendly, older-generation components. While you might need to adjust graphical settings for certain games to run smoothly, our tests showed that esports titles performed admirably at 1080p resolution.

Intel's i3 processors are a great choice for building affordable systems. Although some older AMD Ryzen 5 processors may still be tempting, we believe the CPU selected for this build offers superior price-to-performance ratio.

Points To Consider



Now that you've got a good grasp of how this build performs and what parts it includes, you're probably starting to figure out if it's right for you. But before you make up your mind, there are some important things to think about.

Let's break down the key points you need to consider before deciding whether this build is the right choice for you. By looking at these factors closely, you can make sure it matches your gaming needs perfectly.

So, let's jump in and talk about what you need to know before you buy this build.



$500 Budget


Before diving into any PC purchase, it's crucial to consider your budget. That means asking yourself if you'll be able to build a PC that meets your performance needs with the money you've set aside. Some folks believe that $500 will get them the ultimate gaming rig that can handle VR and 4K gaming at 100FPS. Unfortunately, that's not the reality.

However, we've designed these custom PCs to handle a range of game titles in 1080p resolution. While FPS (frames per second) may vary depending on the game's demands, you can achieve playable FPS if you optimize the settings just right.

Ease Of Building


For first-time builders, it's essential to consider how challenging the assembly process might be. That's why we've chosen a case that's beginner-friendly, with solid build quality to make the build process easier. Surprisingly, cable management was fairly straightforward, and the pre-installed standoff screws helped speed up the entire process.

To assist with assembling the build, we used an iFixit PC building toolkit. Additionally, it's worth noting that for first-time builders, we recommend using an anti-static wristband to eliminate any risk of static damage.if you are new to PC building i encourage you to read our article about common asked question about PC building

Upgradability And Future-Proofing


Another important aspect of a custom PC build is how easily you can upgrade it in the future. As many of you may already be aware, technology is advancing rapidly, and games are becoming more demanding both in terms of computation and storage space. That's why we've designed this PC with upgrades and future-proofing in mind.

Building a PC at this price point can make it challenging to future-proof the setup. However, we've selected a motherboard that can accommodate additional storage drives, more powerful GPUs, better CPUs, and extra RAM if you decide to upgrade later on. This ensures that your PC can adapt to the evolving demands of technology and gaming.

Overclocking


We're all familiar with overclocking—it's a method of boosting your build's performance by manually increasing the clock speed, voltage, and timings of your hardware, all without any extra cost. However, what many people may not realize is that not all hardware is designed for overclocking. Just ask our friendly video guy who recently pushed an entry-level motherboard a bit too far.

When it comes to overclocking your hardware, you need to proceed with caution. Our build is designed for light overclocking and can respond to manual overclocking if you install a better CPU cooler. However, it's worth noting that both of these chips are already quite well optimized, so squeezing out significant real-world performance gains might be a bit tricky.

Non-Gaming Performance


Keep in mind that this build isn't just for gaming, although gaming is its primary focus. We've also considered its capability to handle demanding workstation tasks and multitasking activities. With the powerful CPU and ample RAM included in this build, tasks like live streaming and video editing should be a breeze.

When it comes to browsing and media tasks, this build handles them effortlessly. However, where you might notice a drop in performance is when rendering large video files. If you anticipate doing a lot of that kind of work, we'd suggest upgrading to a more powerful processor.

Aesthetics


Last but not least, let's talk about the aesthetics of the build. In affordable builds, aesthetics often take a back seat, as stylish designs typically come with a higher price tag. Unfortunately, this build features an entry-level case that, to be honest, isn't the most visually appealing case we've come across. However, while it may lack in aesthetics, it makes up for it with its user-friendly design.

$500 Custom Gaming PC vs Prebuilt


Building your own gaming PC typically provides better value, but we understand that not everyone wants to tackle the challenge of building one from scratch. Lately, stock availability has also become a bit of an issue, making it tougher than ever to assemble your own gaming PC. This is where a $500 prebuilt gaming PC might come in handy, but there are a few things to consider first.

Firstly, it's important to realize that you probably won't get the same level of performance from a prebuilt PC at the same price point. If you spend your $500 on a prebuilt system, you'll likely get a lower-end CPU with fewer cores/threads, possibly less RAM and storage capacity, and lower-quality PC cases and components.

This is because prebuilt manufacturers are businesses, and they make a profit by adding a markup (labor costs) to prebuilt computers. While this is understandable, it can affect the overall value you get, especially when every penny counts at the lower end of the price range.

Building Tips


So, here we are at the section of the article where you'll find a bunch of helpful tips if you're a beginner builder.

We understand that there are common mistakes that rookies make when building a PC (it's all part of the learning process), which is why we've put together a guide to help you avoid them. To successfully build your $500 gaming PC, you'll need the right guidance to do it correctly and efficiently. Here are some tips to keep in mind:

  • Build on a secure, non-conductive surface like a wooden table.
  • Assemble your parts in the easiest order—our how-to-build-a-gaming-PC guide can assist you here.
  • Double-check that everything is securely in place before you start testing.
  • Follow the provided manuals for guidance, especially for the motherboard, which usually includes an easy-to-read diagram for inputs.
